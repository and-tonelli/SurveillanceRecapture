Introduction

Bluetongue is a vector-borne viral disease affecting domestic and wild ruminants, caused by the Bluetongue virus (BTV) and transmitted mainly by Culicoides biting midges. In recent years, bluetongue has re-emerged in several European countries, including Italy, posing significant challenges for animal health surveillance systems.

In Italy, information on bluetongue outbreaks is collected through multiple surveillance channels. This report focuses on two data sources (assumed to be independent in this case study):

The National Veterinary Epidemiological Bulletin (BENV), which collects officially notified clinical outbreaks.

EMPRES-i (FAO), a global animal disease information system that aggregates outbreak reports from multiple official and unofficial sources.

Because no single system can be assumed to capture all outbreaks, combining these sources can provide a more realistic estimate of the true number of bluetongue outbreaks. To this end, a captureâ€“recapture approach based on the Chapman estimator was applied.

Methods

Captureâ€“recapture framework

A two-source captureâ€“recapture model was applied at the regional level, using the Chapman estimator, an unbiased correction of the Lincolnâ€“Petersen estimator for small samples.

Let \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) denote the number of outbreaks captured by EMPRES-i and BENV, respectively; while \(m\) is the number of outbreaks observed by both systems.

The Chapman estimator of the total number of outbreaks \(\hat{N}\) is: \[ \hat{N} = \frac{(n_1 + 1)(n_2 + 1)}{(m + 1)} - 1 \] The variance is estimated as: \[ \mathrm{Var}(\hat{N}) = \frac{(n_1 + 1)(n_2 + 1)(n_1 - m)(n_2 - m)} {(m + 1)^2 (m + 2)} \]

From this, 95% confidence intervals were computed assuming approximate normality.

Assumptions

The captureâ€“recapture approach relies on some key assumptions:

Independence of sources: the probability of an outbreak being reported in one system is independent of its reporting in the other.

Homogeneous capture probability within each region.

Conclusions

This analysis shows that combining independent data sources through a captureâ€“recapture framework can provide a more complete picture of outbreak occurrences than either system alone. The results suggest that a non-negligible number of outbreaks may remain unreported in individual surveillance systems.

While the Chapman estimator offers a simple and transparent approach, results should be interpreted cautiously given the underlying assumptions, particularly source independence. Nevertheless, captureâ€“recapture methods represent a valuable tool for strengthening veterinary surveillance and supporting evidence-based animal health decision-making.
